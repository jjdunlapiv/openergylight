Introdution

To demonstrate the benefits of reducing energy demand through better planning and materials, the 173rd Infantry Brigade Combat Team (IBCT) submitted an Operational Needs Statement (ONS) to request PM MEP assist with their set-up and equipment needs in Afghanistan; this support was named Operation Dynamo.

During phase one, eight COPs were chosen to be assessed, upgraded & trained with the latest Environmental Control Units (ECUs), power generating and distribution equipment Power Distribution Illumination System Equipment (PDISE).

The BCT OE Advisor and/or one of four OE Solutions teams traveled to remote COPs and Village Stability Platforms in the Combined Joint Operations Area to assess the conditions, engineer changes, and execute the plans to produce more reliable and efficient electrical power. These changes affected systems supporting life support, command post and sensors on the COP – anything that depends on power. Power improvements are a combat multiplier.

Operation Dynamo

COP power was found to be dangerously inadequate and critical systems were often supported by a band-aid approach to power generation reducing fuel usage.

For example, if more efficient power reduces even one fuel air drop a month, then combat power is returned to the Commander when Soldiers do not have to stand down to recover fuel. Soldiers are available for a higher priority combat mission, and there is less risk to Soldiers in the ground and in the air.

Electrical Power Equioment Planning

Energy distribution plays a dual role in combat effectiveness, it can enable commanders to do more, but it can also tie up resources such as fuel, equipment, and Soldiers to keep powering battle command equipment. PDISE is one solution in providing extended range and freedom of action. By utilizing PDISE you will be able to maximize tactical electrical power usage by maximizing load balancing, reducing the number of generators needed, improving their efficiency and reducing fuel usage.

Tactical electric power generation and distribution equipment must be well planned, supervised, and implemented for maximum efficiency and increase the technical capacity to leverage technology and that can reduce fuel waste and consumption.     Power and energy management is essential to minimize fuel consumption, which reduces logistic support requirements and may provide cost savings within the overall life cycle. Reducing electrical power demand may enable the use of fewer generators, which may produce a more resilient and reliable power system if properly configured into mini- and micro-grids. Cost-effective energy and power management is achieved through a holistic approach that includes implementing:

  • Energy efficient technologies in such things as generators, kitchen equipment, insulation for buildings, and environmental control units (ECUs).
  • Equipment controls such as timers and occupancy sensors.
  • Energy conservation programs that promote behavioral changes in consumers through awareness and leader action.
  • Energy conservation plans.
  • Smart, mini, and micro power distribution grids.
  • Renewable energy resources only after energy and power demands have been minimized.

    Load Requirements

    Military units deploy with a variety of equipment and vehicle types. It is important to collect all equipment load requirements. The load is the total value of power being used by all of the electrical devices that are connected to the PDISE. Computers and other electrical devices may be marked with a voltage and amperage rating, that when multiplied, provides a wattage that the device will use. The total of all of these wattage values provides the amount of power that will be needed.

Each Army unit has an MTOE or a TDA that provides general information about its mission and employment. It also contains very detailed information about its personnel and equipment numbers and types. While difficult to read and understand, these documents can provide valuable information if direct unit coordination is not possible.

    Phase Load Balance

    Phase balancing is an important step in the power distribution process. Remember, the M200, M100, and M40 PDISE systems use a three-phase input from the power source. When connecting any three phase load to these systems, the load is connected across each phase and will be automatically balanced on all phases.

    When connecting single-phase loads to these systems, the loads need to be placed across the phases in a balanced manner, meaning the total value of the current or amperage should be spread across the phases so that the phases have equal current values. Remember that the generator maintains voltage adjustment by monitoring phase C, so it should contain the highest load.

    It is crucial that each phase of the generator set is evenly loaded so that the circuits within the set are not overloaded or placed in an unstable condition. If this occurs, the safety devices within the generator set would cause it to shut down.

    Within tent number 1 are two separate single-phase circuits that need to be placed on the output connectors of the M40 PDISE supporting that tent. One circuit has a total load of 16 amps, and the other has a total of 5 amps.

    Tent number 2 has four separate single-phase circuits that need to be placed on the output connectors of the M40 PDISE supporting that tent. One circuit has a total load of 2 amps, another has a total of 9 amps, another has a total of 11 amps, and the last has a total of 4 amps.

    A solution for connecting these loads is to place the 16 amp circuit in tent 1 on a connector of the M40 supporting tent 1 that links to phase C. Then place the remaining 5 amp circuit on phase B.

    Now, place the 11 amp circuit in tent 2 on a connector of that M40 which links to phase B. This now means that phases B and C have total loads of 16 amps each as seen at the generator set. Now, place the remaining circuits from tent 2 on phase A, for a total load of 15 amps.


    This solution provides a balanced load across the phases of the generator set and does not overload any of the individual connector circuit breakers. There could be several solutions but the goal is to always balance the load across the phases without overloading circuits.

    Typically, electrical devices have labels or tags that provide power rating information. You will need to physically obtain this data from each device by locating the device and looking at its data plate or by obtaining that information from the devices technical manual..

    The data found on the device labels can be presented in several ways. Devices such as lights, heaters, and coffee pots are known as resistive loads and will normally have power and voltage ratings. Computers and other electronic equipment may include the voltage and the current rating for the device.

    Note that most computers will provide a current rating over a range of voltages, usually 100-240 V. Use the maximum current draw at the 100 V rating to calculate the power required for the item.

    Select TEPE

    Tactical Electrical Power Equipment is the primary source for electric power in the tactical battlefield and it is a critical element in maintaining a superior military force. In order to provide the many power consuming devices with adequate power, a power distribution grid needs to be properly configured. The PDISE family of power distribution equipment provides the ability to safely and efficiently distribute power where it is needed.

    Generator sets are designed to operate at various voltages, frequencies, and power levels. To meet a particular power demand, an operator must choose a set with the proper characteristics. If a large set is needed but is unavailable, several small sets, each located near the load to be supplied, may be used. Additional operators and maintenance personnel may be required if several small sets replace a large one.

    Selecting generators that can produce the power required by a field unit is an important function. The operator or person responsible for this function must select the number and types of generators that can best meet the unit's needs. Preliminary tasks that must be completed before selecting power-generating equipment are computing the load, computing the cable size, and balancing the load required for the field unit.

    After you have designed the distribution system and balanced the load, you can select the generating equipment to produce the power needed for the field unit. The electrical systems at most military field units supply power day and night for various lighting, heating, and power equipment.

    The annual load factor of a well-operated, active field unit is 50 percent or more of the capacity of the generator sets. The annual load has a power factor of 80 percent or more of the average power factor. The following criteria govern the generator selection process:

  • Electrical loads to be supplied.
  • Kilowatt rating requirements.
  • Operating voltages required.
  • Number of phases required.
  • Frequency requirements

    • Other considerations when selecting generating equipment for a field unit include—

  • Availability of fuels.
  • Expected life of the field unit.
  • Availability of skilled maintenance personnel.
  • Probable load deviation.

  • Three-phase equipment provides power for almost everything except small loads. The generation and transmission lines are usually three-wire systems, but the distribution circuits may be three for four-wire. When single-phase power is obtained from three-phase circuits, operators must balance each phase at the generator set.
  • Lighting is universally rated at 120 volts in the US. Using both lighting and small-motor circuits increases the load requirements for general power applications.

    The principal power distribution system for the Army is called the Power Distribution Illumination System Electrical -- or PDISE . PDISE is basically a more rugged version of home distribution boxes/panels (with various size circuits and circuit overload protection), plus the connecting cables and a utility subsystem. PDISE is designed to facilitate a safe power grid, in the past it has been difficult and power mangers had problems with improperly setting up a power grid that led to inefficient utilization of power sources, excessive power outages that caused equipment failure, safety hazards that harm users a unnecessary fuel consumption.

    PDISE is a family of electrical circuit boxes that safely limit the amount of power flowing from a power source to the electrical devices, also known as the load. This protects the generator set from becoming overloaded and maintains a safe working environment for the user and the load devices. PDISE also consists of the cabling and outlet boxes that are needed to develop a complete circuit of power.

    DISE/PDISE consists of items used to form a consolidated power network by adding components to meet specific organizational mission and requirements. DISE/PDISE provides flexibility to unit operations and can be quickly assembled or disassembled for rapid relocation. Line distance from generator to load is limited to 300 feet at maximum load, because distance greater than 300 feet would cause an unacceptable voltage loss.

    Each center is equipped with a master circuit breaker rated at the maximum permissible load. Each center is equipped with phase-indicator lights that indicate the presence of input power. Each center is equipped with branch circuits that are protected by isolated circuit breakers.

    The quantity, location, and placement of PDISE equipment are dependent upon field topology and conditions, and user needs. A typical tactical power grid consists of a power source, which is usually a generator set, that is connected to a network of PDISE. The PDISE then distributes the power through various cables and assemblages of utility extension cords and branch circuits to the electrical loads.

    PDISE Characteristics

    PDISE can be thought of as a more rugged version of a typical home distribution panel that is designed to be portable and quickly assembled and disassembled, permitting rapid relocation and flexibility in configuring the power grid. PDISE equipment can be used in all weather conditions and will operate at temperatures ranging from -25°F to 140°F and at all altitudes. If special precautions are taken, PDISE equipment can operate in conditions of extreme cold or extreme heat and in environments that are extremely rainy and humid, or extremely dusty and sandy.

    PDISE comes in 4 sizes of distribution boxes (40, 60, 100, and 200 Amps), connecting cables, and assemblage of utility extension cords, branch circuits and lighting equipment. Supports mobility by enabling quick assemble/dissemble. Various sizes allow selection to match generator sets from 5kW to 100kW.

    For the M200, the total load for any combination of branch circuits must not exceed 72kW. The individual branch circuit output loads must not exceed 36kW for the 100-amp 3-phase branch circuits, 21.6kW for the 60-amp 3-phase branch circuits, or 2.4kW for the 20-amp single-phase branch circuit.

    For the M100, the total load for any combination of branch circuits must not exceed 36kW. The individual branch circuit output loads must not exceed 36kW for the 100-amp 3-phase branch circuit, 21.6kW for the 60-amp 3-phase branch circuits, 14.4kW for the 40-amp 3-phase branch circuits, or 2.4kW for each of the 20-amp single-phase branch circuit.

    For the M40, the total load for any combination of branch circuits must not exceed 14.4kW. The individual branch circuit output loads must not exceed 14.4kW for the 40-amp 3-phase branch circuit, or 2.4kW for each of the 20-amp single-phase branch circuits.

    For the M60, the total load must not exceed 7.2 kW and each of the individual branch circuit loads must not exceed 2.4kW.

    The PDISE line of equipment consists of four individual systems that control and limit the amount of current or amps being drawn from a power source. These systems are broken into Feeder Systems, which are intended to route current to other PDISE, and Distribution Systems, which are intended to route current to the load. Note that Feeder Systems can also route current to a load, but care must be taken not to overload the system if it is also feeding current to other PDISE.

    Feeder Systems include the M200 PDISE and the M100 PDISE. They limit current to 200 amps and 100 amps respectively, and are a 3 phase system that requires electrical power rated at 208VAC, 50/60Hz. These systems will provide 3 phase 208VAC, 50/60Hz or single phase 120VAC, 50/60Hz electrical power depending on which output connectors are used. Distribution Systems include the M40 PDISE and the M60 PDISE. These limit current to 40 amps and 60 amps respectively.

    The M40 system is a 3 phase system that requires electrical power rated at 120/208VAC, 50/60Hz. Like the M200 and M100, it will output 3 phase 208VAC, 50/60Hz or single phase 120VAC, 50/60Hz electrical power depending on which output connectors are used. The M60 is a single phase system that requires electrical power rated at 120VAC, 50/60Hz for operating single phase 120VAC, 50/60Hz loads.

    The M46 Electrical Utility Assembly Kit can be used to provide lighting and 120VAC duplex receptacle outlets to field shelters. The M46 kit contains a rugged carrying case, various electrical cables for extending electrical circuits to the locations of the load devices, fluorescent lights for illumination, rugged duplex receptacles that are used for plugging in electrical loads, and various items such as ropes and hook that are used to secure these items to the inside of shelters. The provided electrical cables connect to the single-phase outputs of any PDISE system and distribute the duplex receptacles and lighting into the field shelter.

    The M46 kit is not intended to replace the shelters existing lighting sets, but is intended to augment and expand current capabilities. The amount of outlets required for plugging in all load devices will determine the amount of M46 kits needed. The universal adapter is designed to connect electrical equipment without military standard connectors to the electrical power distribution equipment. The universal adapter consists of a box with a 5- pin, 3-phase 40/60 amp input connector and five terminals. The box includes an insulated wrench for tightening/loosening the terminals.

    PDISE Setup

    Before emplacing TEPE, the power plant must be located where the TEPE system components can reach all areas of the CP. The M40 PDISE is normally positioned between the Current Ops and Sustainment cells but it can be positioned as required to suit the CP application.

    Generally, PDISE are arranged in small, medium, or large configurations. A small configuration usually consists of a smaller sized power source, normally in the range of a 3kW to 10kW, and sometimes even as high as a 15kW generator set that provides power to a single or small group of shelters with minimal electrical devices.

    A medium configuration usually consists of a midsized power source, normally in the range of a 15kW to 30kW, and sometimes a 60kW generator set that provides power to a group of shelters with a moderate amount of electrical devices. This configuration could resemble a small medical facility, or a normal sized command center with a few surrounding support operations.

    A larger configuration usually consists of a large sized power source, normally in the range of a 60kW to 100kW generator set that provides power to multiple shelters with a significant amount of electrical equipment. This configuration could resemble a larger medical facility or small hospital, or a large command center with many supporting operations.
